The Chernobyl Nuclear Power Plant was built in the former Soviet Union, in the now independent country of Ukraine and about 20 km south of Belarus (1). The specific location of the reactor was chosen for economic reasons, so that the energy would not have to travel far distances before reaching its target consumers (1). Specifically, the power plant was 3 km away from the 49,000 residents of Priypat city and 15 km away from the 12,500 residents of Chernobyl (1). Within a 30 kilometer radius of the power plant, there were residential areas that totaled between 115,000 to 135,000 people (3). In order to house the workers of the power plant, the city of Pripyat was constructed in 1970 (2). At the time of its construction, the city attracted workers and residents as it was considered the most modern and advanced city in the entirety of the Soviet Union (2). Unlike most other nuclear reactors, the Chernobyl Nuclear Power plant was graphite-moderated instead of water-moderated (1). This meant that the reactors used graphite moderators and control rods to slow down neutrons and manage the activation and deactivation of the nuclear chain reaction. In particular, there were two main design flaws in the building of the power plant that lead to the ontogenesis of the nuclear disaster.
